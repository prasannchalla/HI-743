# Obesity Prediction Model
# 1. Introduction
Obesity has become a significant public health issue, affecting millions of individuals globally and leading to various chronic diseases, such as heart disease, diabetes, stroke, and certain types of cancer. In the United States, obesity rates have increased substantially over the past few decades, and the problem continues to worsen. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), nearly 40% of adults in the U.S. are classified as obese, with serious implications for public health, healthcare systems, and overall well-being.
In Lake County, Illinois, obesity remains a major concern due to its far-reaching effects on health outcomes and healthcare costs. Socioeconomic factors, such as income, education, physical activity levels, and access to healthy food, all play a role in influencing obesity rates. Understanding these factors is vital for implementing effective health interventions. The goal of this project is to develop a predictive model to identify key factors that influence obesity rates and predict its prevalence in specific populations. Such a model could serve as a tool for policymakers, healthcare providers, and local government agencies to allocate resources more efficiently and design targeted interventions.
By accurately predicting obesity rates, public health organizations can implement preventative measures aimed at reducing obesity levels, leading to better health outcomes. Through this model, stakeholders can identify communities at higher risk, allocate health resources, and take steps to reduce healthcare costs related to obesity.
# 2. Research Questions and Hypotheses
The central research question of this study is: Can demographic, socioeconomic, and health-related factors accurately predict obesity rates in Lake County, Illinois, and at the national level
Target Variable: The target variable in this analysis is Obesity Status, which is a binary classification variable. We define obesity status as follows:
Obese (1): Individuals with obesity, as defined by a Body Mass Index (BMI) greater than 30.
Not Obese (0): Individuals without obesity.
Predictors (Independent Variables): The predictors used to forecast obesity status include various demographic and socioeconomic factors that have been shown in previous studies to influence obesity rates. These predictors are:
Income Level: Socioeconomic status is a significant factor in determining access to healthier food options and opportunities for physical activity. Individuals with higher income levels often have better access to healthier foods and healthcare services, which may influence their likelihood of being obese.
Education Level: Higher levels of education are associated with better knowledge of healthy lifestyle choices, including diet and physical activity. This may reduce the likelihood of obesity in individuals with higher education levels.
Physical Activity: Regular physical activity is a known determinant in reducing obesity rates. A higher level of physical activity is expected to correlate with a lower likelihood of obesity.
Geographic Factors (Area and Length): The location (area) and geography (shape length) can provide insights into how urban versus rural environments might influence obesity rates, where access to fitness centers, grocery stores, and healthcare services may vary.

# Hypotheses:
Hypothesis 1: Higher income levels are associated with lower obesity rates. Individuals with higher incomes tend to have access to healthier food options, can afford gym memberships, and have better access to healthcare services that promote healthier lifestyles. Thus, we expect that higher income levels will correlate with lower obesity rates.
Hypothesis 2: Higher education levels are inversely related to obesity rates. Individuals with higher education levels are often more informed about the risks of obesity and tend to make healthier lifestyle choices. As a result, we expect to see a correlation between higher education levels and lower obesity rates.
# 3. Methodology
The methodology section outlines the machine learning techniques used to build the predictive model, the rationale behind the choice of models, and the steps taken to preprocess the data.
# 3.1 Data Collection and Preprocessing
The dataset used for this project is the LakeCounty_Health.csv, which contains public health data for Lake County, Illinois, focusing on obesity rates and associated demographic and socioeconomic factors. This data is sourced from the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS), a national health survey conducted by the CDC, which collects data on health-related risk behaviors, chronic health conditions, and the use of preventive services.
Before building the model, we conducted several preprocessing steps:
Handling Missing Values: Missing values were checked, and since there were no missing values in the dataset, no further action was required.
Data Transformation: A binary classification variable for obesity status was created. If the obesity rate exceeded 30%, the status was labeled as "Obese" (1); otherwise, it was labeled as "Not Obese" (0). This allowed us to frame the prediction task as a binary classification problem.
Feature Selection: The predictors used in the model include demographic and socioeconomic variables such as income level, education level, physical activity, and geographic factors (Shape__Area and Shape__Length). The target variable was the binary obesity status.
Splitting the Data: The dataset was divided into a training set (80%) and a test set (20%) to train and evaluate the model.
# 3.2 Model Selection
Two machine learning models were chosen for this project: Support Vector Machine (SVM) and Decision Tree.
Support Vector Machine (SVM): The SVM model was chosen due to its ability to handle non-linear decision boundaries, which may be present in the relationship between demographic and socioeconomic factors and obesity rates. Using the Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel, SVM can transform the feature space into higher dimensions, allowing for the identification of complex relationships between the predictors and the target variable. SVM is particularly effective in binary classification problems and is known for its robustness in high-dimensional spaces.
Decision Tree: The decision tree was selected for its simplicity and interpretability. Decision trees work by partitioning the data based on feature values, resulting in a tree-like structure. Each node in the tree represents a decision rule, and the leaf nodes represent the final classification. Decision trees are effective in handling both numerical and categorical data, making them highly versatile. Additionally, decision trees do not require feature scaling and are easy to interpret, which makes them a good choice for this type of problem.
# 3.3 Model Evaluation
After training both models on the training dataset, the models were evaluated using various performance metrics, including accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, and confusion matrices. The results were analyzed to determine which model performed best.
# 4. Results
The performance of the models was evaluated using confusion matrices and accuracy scores. The SVM model achieved an accuracy of 80%, showing a good balance between sensitivity and specificity. However, it struggled with correctly identifying the "Not Obese" class, as evidenced by its low specificity. The Decision Tree model outperformed the SVM with a perfect accuracy score of 100%. It identified all instances of both "Obese" and "Not Obese" accurately, but this might indicate overfitting. Overfitting occurs when a model performs well on the training data but fails to generalize to new, unseen data.
# 5. Conclusion
In this project, we successfully developed two machine learning models to predict obesity status based on demographic and socioeconomic factors. While the Decision Tree model achieved perfect accuracy, it may have overfit the training data, making it less reliable for generalization. On the other hand, the SVM model demonstrated robust performance, with 80% accuracy, but could benefit from improvements in specificity.
The Decision Tree model is an excellent tool for understanding the relationship between predictors and the target variable, while the SVM model is more appropriate for capturing non-linear relationships. Future improvements, such as hyperparameter tuning, cross-validation, and exploring other machine learning algorithms, could enhance the models’ generalization capabilities.
The final model will serve as a valuable tool for policymakers and public health officials in predicting obesity rates and designing targeted interventions to reduce obesity and improve public health outcomes.
